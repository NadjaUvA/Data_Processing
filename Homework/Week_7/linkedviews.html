The ageing of the population is a wide-spread problem nowadays. In the past decades, the number of elderly (65 years and older) has increased, while the number of young people (14 years and younger) has decreased. This phenomenon is also noticable in Northwestern Europe, where countries are struggling with issues like a non-sufficient pension budget. By clicking on a bar, the development of the past decades of a country is shown. By checking the boxes under "countries", the population composition can be compared between countries. This decomposition is based on the most recent data values of the OECD on this topic.

The data supports the fact that the number of young people is declining for every Northwestern-European country. For most countries it also supports the fact that the number of elderly people is increasing. Only in Ireland and Luxembourg the percentage of the elderly population is relatively constant. In Sweden, the percentage of elderly and young people has been about the same since 1985, while showing the stated trends a bit. In almost all countries it seems that in the last few years, the precentage of elderly people is catching up on the percentage of younger people. However, in Germany, the percentage of elderly people has been higher since the eighties. All in all, I can conclude that the data supports the expectations made upfront.
